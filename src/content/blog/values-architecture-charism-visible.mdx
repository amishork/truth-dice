# Values Architecture: Making Your Charism Visible

Every Catholic school has a charism — the specific spiritual gift of its founding order or community. Franciscan schools emphasize simplicity and care for creation. Jesuit schools emphasize intellectual rigor and discernment. Dominican schools emphasize truth-seeking. Augustinian schools emphasize community and interiority.

But walk into most Catholic schools and you'll struggle to see the charism in action. You'll see crucifixes. You'll see a mission statement. You might see the founder's portrait in the main hall. What you probably won't see is a visible, operational framework that connects the charism to daily life — that tells students, faculty, and parents exactly what this school values and how those values show up.

That framework is what I call values architecture.

## What Values Architecture Is

Values architecture is the visible, shareable structure that connects a school's founding identity to its lived culture. It answers three questions that a mission statement alone cannot:

**What do we value?** A short list of three to five specific commitments, named in language that students can understand and adults can operationalize.

**What does each value look like?** For each value, concrete descriptions of what it looks like in the classroom, in the hallways, in athletics, in discipline, and in community life. Observable, not aspirational.

**How do we form toward these values?** The specific programs, practices, rituals, and systems that make each value the default experience of being at this school.

## Why Charism Alone Isn't Enough

A charism is a theological gift. It's profound, but it's also abstract. "Franciscan simplicity" is a beautiful concept. But a fourteen-year-old needs to know what simplicity means when she's choosing how to treat a classmate, or when her teacher is designing a service project, or when the administration is making a budget decision.

Values architecture translates the charism into operational language. It takes the theological gift and builds the educational infrastructure around it. The charism remains the source — but the architecture makes it visible, teachable, and accountable.

## Building the Architecture

The process begins with excavation: what does this charism actually mean for this community, in this place, at this time? This is a formation question, not a research question. It requires conversation with faculty, administrators, students, parents, and often with members of the founding order.

From that conversation, a small number of core values emerge. The charism translated into commitments that can be observed, taught, and measured.

Each value then gets operationalized. What does it look like in a freshman advisory session? In a varsity practice? In a parent meeting? In a hiring interview? The architecture is complete when every stakeholder can point to specific practices that embody each value — and when those practices are designed, not accidental.

## The Visible Result

A school with strong values architecture doesn't need to tell you what it stands for. You can see it in the language students use with each other, in how teachers handle disruption, in what gets celebrated at assemblies, in the questions administrators ask before making decisions.

The charism moves from the portrait in the hallway to the texture of the day. That's values architecture.
