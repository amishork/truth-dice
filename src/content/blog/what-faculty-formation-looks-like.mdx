# What Faculty Formation Actually Looks Like

Most Catholic schools invest heavily in professional development. Teachers attend workshops on differentiated instruction, assessment strategies, technology integration, and classroom management. They earn continuing education credits. They stay current.

Almost none of this is formation.

Professional development makes teachers better at their craft. Formation makes teachers better at their _vocation_. The distinction matters because a school's mission is not "teach well" — it's "form human beings." And you cannot form what you have not been formed in yourself.

## The Uncomfortable Truth

Faculty who have never done their own values work will default to transmitting their personal preferences, their cultural assumptions, or their institutional compliance habits. They'll teach content. They'll manage behavior. They'll care about students — genuinely. But they will not form students toward specific, articulated values unless someone has first helped them do that work for themselves.

This is not a criticism of teachers. It's a criticism of the system that asks them to be formators without forming them first.

## What Formation Looks Like in Practice

Faculty formation is not a retreat day, though it might include one. It's not a book study, though reading together can be part of it. It's a sustained, structured process that does three things.

**It surfaces what faculty actually value.** Using structured assessments and facilitated conversation, each teacher identifies their own core values — not their aspirational values or their school's values, but the commitments that actually drive their decisions. This is uncomfortable work. It reveals gaps. That's the point.

**It connects personal values to institutional mission.** Once teachers know what they value, they can examine where their values align with the school's charism — and where tension exists. This is not about conformity. It's about integrity. A teacher who values excellence in a school that values compassion needs to understand how those interact. A teacher who values independence in a school that values community needs to see where that creates friction.

**It builds shared vocabulary and practice.** The most important outcome of faculty formation is a shared language. When every adult in a building can name the same three to five values and point to what they look like in practice, students experience consistency. They hear the same words in English class and in the gym. They see the same expectations in the dean's office and the art studio. Consistency is the medium of formation.

## The Ripple Effect

Formed faculty change the culture of a school faster than any program, curriculum, or policy change. When a teacher who has done her own values work encounters a struggling student, she doesn't just apply a behavior protocol — she draws on a shared framework that connects the student's behavior to the community's commitments. When a department chair has the vocabulary to talk about formation, faculty meetings become more than logistical updates.

The investment is not large. A semester of monthly formation sessions — 90 minutes each — is enough to build the foundation. The return is a faculty that doesn't just teach the mission. They embody it.
