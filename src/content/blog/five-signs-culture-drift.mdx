# Five Signs Your School's Culture Is Drifting From Its Mission

Culture drift is quiet. It doesn't announce itself. There's no moment when a school's culture breaks — it erodes. Slowly, decision by decision, hire by hire, the lived experience of being at the school diverges from the stated mission. By the time leadership notices, the gap is significant.

Here are five diagnostic signals that the drift has begun.

## 1. Faculty can't connect their work to the mission without prompting.

Ask a teacher — any teacher — how their lesson today connects to the school's mission. If the answer requires a long pause, a generous reinterpretation, or the phrase "I mean, in a way, everything we do connects to the mission," the mission is not operational. It's decorative.

This isn't a teacher problem. It's a system problem. If the school hasn't built the bridge between charism and classroom practice — through formation, shared vocabulary, and designed experiences — individual teachers have no framework for making the connection. They'll default to their discipline, their pedagogy, and their personal values. Those may be excellent. But they won't be unified.

## 2. Discipline is punitive rather than formative.

How a school handles misbehavior reveals what it actually values. A school that responds to infractions with escalating consequences — detention, suspension, expulsion — is prioritizing order. A school that responds with conversation, reflection, and restoration is prioritizing formation.

Neither approach is wrong in isolation. But if the mission statement says "we form the whole person" and the discipline system says "comply or face consequences," students learn to read the system, not the statement. The system always wins.

Formative discipline asks: what value was violated? Does the student understand that value? What experience would help them internalize it? These questions take more time than a referral form. They also produce more lasting results.

## 3. New hires aren't screened for mission alignment.

The hiring process reveals institutional priorities with perfect clarity. If every interview question is about qualifications, experience, and pedagogical philosophy — and none are about values, formation, or mission — the school is hiring for competence, not culture.

Competence matters. But a school can recover from a competence gap through training. A values misalignment is much harder to address after the contract is signed. Schools experiencing culture drift almost always have a hiring process that optimizes for credentials over character.

The fix is straightforward: add two or three values-based questions to every interview. Not "Do you support our mission?" — everyone says yes. Instead: "Tell me about a time you had to choose between efficiency and care for a student. What did you do and why?" The answer reveals values in action.

## 4. Parent engagement means fundraising, not formation.

If the primary way parents interact with the school is through tuition payments, fundraising events, and booster clubs, the school has a customer relationship with families, not a formative one. This is a significant drift signal because Catholic education claims to be a partnership between school and home. If that partnership is primarily financial, the claim is hollow.

Formative parent engagement looks different. It includes parents in the values conversation. It offers family formation experiences that extend the school's mission into the household. It creates space for parents to do their own values work, not just support their child's academic progress.

## 5. The school's best moments are accidental, not designed.

Every school has moments of genuine formation — a retreat where something real happens, a classroom conversation that changes how a student sees the world, a faculty meeting where the mission suddenly feels alive. The question is whether those moments are designed or accidental.

In a school with strong values architecture, formation moments are built into the structure. Advisory programs are designed around specific values. Retreats have a clear formation arc. Faculty meetings include formation, not just logistics. The moments happen because the system produces them.

In a drifting school, those moments are serendipitous. They depend on exceptional individuals, not on institutional design. When that teacher retires or that campus minister moves on, the moments go with them. That's drift — not because anything broke, but because nothing was built to last.

## The Path Back

Culture drift is reversible. The path back starts with honest diagnosis — looking at these five signals without defensiveness — and continues with intentional design. The mission doesn't need to be rewritten. It needs to be rebuilt into the daily architecture of the school.

That's formation work. And it's the most important investment a school can make.
